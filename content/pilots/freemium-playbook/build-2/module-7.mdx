# Keeping Them (Churn Prevention)

Every community owner eventually faces the same math problem. You add 20 new members. 8 cancel. You add 15 more. 6 cancel. You're running to stay in place, and every month the treadmill gets a little faster.

Churn is the tax on everything you've built. Your content strategy, your live sessions, your onboarding, your challenges; none of it compounds if members are leaving as fast as they arrive.

<KeyData>
The average Skool community churns 18% of paid members per month. That's roughly 1 in 5 members leaving every month. Under 10% is considered good. One in ten. Even the "good" number means you're replacing 10% of your revenue base every single month. (Sources: Sam O from Skool team, Skool Educate)
</KeyData>

Hamza Ahmed's Adonis School generates approximately $193,000/mo in MRR. But his churn rate is 30-40%. At that rate, he needs a constant flood of new members just to stay flat. He publicly asked for help with this in mid-2024, saying he'd tried more calls, more posts, more courses. Nothing moved the number.

This module is about building a community people don't want to leave, not a content library they finish and cancel.

## What You'll Build

By the end of this module, you have four things working:

1. **A churn dashboard.** Four metrics you track weekly that tell you whether your community is healthy or bleeding.

2. **A re-engagement system.** A process for spotting quiet members and reaching out before they cancel.

3. **A monthly value reminder.** A brief, proof-rich summary that shows members what they've accessed and achieved.

4. **A content strategy that prevents fatigue.** An approach to posting that keeps members engaged without overwhelming them.

## Section 1: Understanding Your Churn

### What the numbers actually look like

Let's set the baseline with real data.

<KeyData>
**General subscription churn:** The average monthly churn rate across subscription businesses is 5.3% (Recurly benchmark data). For context, that means even well-run subscription businesses lose about 1 in 20 customers every month.

**Paid communities specifically:** Monthly churn for paid communities hovers between 5-10% (CommuniPass). That's slightly better than subscription businesses overall, which tells you something important: community creates stickiness that standalone products don't.

**Skool specifically:** Average churn is 18% per month, per Sam O from the Skool team. Under 10% is good. New communities often start at 30% before optimizing down to roughly 8% (Skool Educate). If you're at 20%+ after your first three months, your retention system needs work before you focus on growth.
</KeyData>

<KeyData>
**First-year members vs. established members:** First-year members renew at 75% vs. 84% for established members, a 9-point gap (Marketing General Inc. 2025 Benchmarking Report). This confirms what Module 5 covered: onboarding is your highest-leverage retention tool. Close the first-year gap and your overall churn drops significantly.
</KeyData>

### The two types of churn

**Voluntary churn** is when a member actively decides to cancel. They click the cancel button. This is the churn you can influence through better onboarding, content, and community connection.

**Involuntary churn** is when a payment fails (expired card, insufficient funds). The member didn't decide to leave; their payment method broke. This is surprisingly common and entirely preventable with saved payment methods and auto-retry systems. Most payment processors handle this automatically, but check that your Skool payment settings aren't letting failed payments become silent cancellations.

### Your churn dashboard

Track these four metrics weekly. They tell different stories about your community's health.

**1. Monthly churn rate**
Formula: (members lost this month / members at start of month) x 100
Target: under 10%

**2. Engagement rate**
Formula: (members who posted, commented, or reacted this week / total paid members) x 100
Target: above 50% weekly

<Insight>
Engagement rate is your leading indicator. Churn is a lagging indicator; by the time someone cancels, the decision happened weeks ago. Engagement rate drops before churn rate rises. If engagement drops from 60% to 40% over two weeks, you have a churn problem forming even if nobody has cancelled yet.
</Insight>

**3. Time since last activity**
For each paid member: how many days since they last posted, commented, or reacted?
Warning threshold: 14+ days of silence

**4. Net member growth**
Formula: new paid members this month minus cancelled paid members
Target: positive every month

<WatchOut>
If net member growth is negative for two consecutive months, stop all growth activities and focus entirely on retention. Adding members to a leaking bucket is the most expensive mistake community owners make.
</WatchOut>

## Section 2: Why Members Actually Leave

### The real reasons

The instinct when a member cancels is to think "I need to create more content" or "I need to lower my price." Both instincts are usually wrong.

Here's what the research says about why members leave:

<KeyData>
**Lack of engagement is the number one reason.** Higher Logic's research across hundreds of membership organizations consistently finds that members who don't engage are the ones who leave. The mechanism is simple: if a member isn't participating, they're not experiencing value. If they're not experiencing value, they can't justify the cost. This isn't about the quality of your content. It's about whether the member is actually using it.
</KeyData>

<KeyData>
**Subscription fatigue is real, but misunderstood.** Deloitte's 2025 Digital Media Trends survey found that the average U.S. household cut subscriptions from 4.1 services in 2024 to 2.8 in 2025, a 32% drop. People are actively consolidating their subscriptions. This means your community isn't just competing with other communities. It's competing with Netflix, Spotify, meal kits, and every other recurring charge on the credit card statement. Your community needs to feel essential, not optional, when the member reviews their monthly expenses.
</KeyData>

<Insight>
**Content fatigue drives cancellation through overwhelm.** This is the counterintuitive one. More content can increase churn. 41% of consumers report that content overwhelm influences their decision to cancel subscriptions (Deloitte, original stat SVOD-specific; principle applies broadly). Decision fatigue and cognitive load from too much content make the community feel like work instead of value. When checking in feels like homework, members stop checking in.
</Insight>

<KeyData>
**Lack of community connection.** Members connected to other members churn at significantly lower rates than members connected only to the founder. Subscriptions with community features reduce churn by 23% compared to subscriptions without them (Churnkey State of Retention 2025). The community IS the retention mechanism, not a feature of the subscription.
</KeyData>

### The content treadmill trap

This deserves its own subsection because it's the most common mistake.

When engagement drops, the natural response is to create more content. More posts, more lessons, more recordings, more resources. The thinking: if members had more to consume, they'd engage more.

<WatchOut>
The research says the opposite. More than one new content piece per week increases overwhelm and churn (MembershipGeeks). The fix is not more content; it's more community interaction. Once members build relationships with each other, content pressure drops because the value isn't in what you publish. It's in the conversations that happen around it.
</WatchOut>

<Insight>
This is why Module 6 (live sessions) and Module 5 (onboarding that creates connections) come before this module. If your onboarding connects members to each other and your live sessions create regular interaction, your content can be focused and sustainable rather than exhausting.
</Insight>

## Section 3: The Re-engagement System

### Catching members before they cancel

Module 5 covered the early warning signals for churn (active frustration, disengagement, silent withdrawal). This section builds the system for acting on those signals.

<Action>
**Weekly scan (5 minutes)**

Every week, scan your paid member list for:
- Members who haven't posted or commented in 14+ days
- Members who attended live sessions regularly but stopped
- Members whose engagement pattern shifted (used to post 3x/week, now 0)

On Skool, the leaderboard and recent activity feed give you this information at a glance. You don't need analytics software. You need the habit of looking.
</Action>

### The outreach template

When you spot a quiet member, send a personal DM. Not a "we miss you!" mass message. A personal, specific message:

<Action>
"Hey [name], I noticed you haven't been around much lately. No pressure at all. I wanted to check in because I remember you were working on [specific thing they mentioned]. How's that going? Is there something you're looking for that you're not finding here?"

This message works because:
- It references something specific they shared (proves you're paying attention)
- It asks an open question (gives them space to tell you what's wrong)
- It doesn't mention their subscription or payment
- It gives them an easy out without burning the bridge
</Action>

### What the responses tell you

Members who respond to your outreach fall into three categories:

1. **"Life got busy."** They appreciate the check-in and often re-engage. No action needed beyond the message.

2. **"I'm not finding what I need."** This is gold. They're telling you what's missing. Listen, adjust, and follow up. These members can become your most loyal advocates if you act on their feedback.

3. **No response.** After 7 days with no reply, send one more brief message: "Just wanted to make sure this landed. No obligation to respond, but if there's anything I can do, I'm here." After that, let it go. Some members will quietly cancel regardless.

### When NOT to chase

<Insight>
Not every cancellation is preventable, and not every member is worth chasing. Members who joined for a specific resource, got it, and left were never going to be long-term members. Members who joined because of a promotion and never engaged weren't a fit. Chasing low-fit members burns your energy and doesn't move your churn rate meaningfully.

Focus your re-engagement effort on members who were active and went quiet. That's where the leverage is.
</Insight>

## Section 4: The Monthly Value Reminder

### Why members forget what they're getting

Here's a pattern that drives churn: a member has been in your community for three months. They've attended live sessions, completed a challenge, had two great conversations in the feed. But when they see the $29 charge on their credit card, they think: "What am I getting from this?"

The problem isn't a lack of value. It's a lack of visibility. Members forget what they've consumed. They don't mentally tally the sessions attended, resources downloaded, or connections made. They just see the recurring charge and run a vibe check: "Does this feel worth it?"

Your job is to make the answer to that question easy and obvious.

### The monthly ROI post

Once a month, post a summary in the community feed (or the paid-only section) that recaps what happened. This isn't a newsletter. It's a proof summary.

<Action>
**Template:**

**This month in [community name]:**

What we built:
- [Member] shipped [thing] using the framework from Module [X]
- [Member] completed the [challenge name] and got [result]
- [Number] members participated in live sessions

What we covered:
- Week 1: [topic] (recording in classroom)
- Week 2: [topic]
- Week 3: [topic]
- Week 4: [topic]

Coming next month:
- [Upcoming challenge or event]
- [New content planned]
- [Guest or collaboration]

If you have a win from this month, drop it in the comments. Your results become part of the proof ledger.
</Action>

This post does multiple things:
- Reminds members of the value they received (even if they forgot)
- Showcases member wins (social proof for retention)
- Creates anticipation for next month (reduces "I'll cancel and maybe rejoin later" thinking)
- Feeds the proof ledger from the Proof-Based Communication module

### The personal touch (for premium members)

If you have a small paid tier (under 30 members), consider a personal monthly DM to each paid member:

"Hey [name], quick monthly check-in. This month you [attended X sessions / completed Y / posted about Z]. Anything specific you're hoping to get from the community next month? I'm planning [upcoming thing] and want to make sure it's relevant."

This takes 10-15 minutes for 20 members. The return on that time, in terms of reduced churn, is significant.

## Section 5: Annual Billing as a Churn Tool

### The math

Annual billing reduces churn because it changes the decision architecture. Instead of 12 monthly decisions about whether to stay, the member makes one annual decision. Every decision point is a potential exit point. Fewer decision points means fewer exits.

<KeyData>
General subscription data supports this: annual plan churn rates are 9.5 percentage points lower than monthly plans (Recurly). The mechanism is straightforward. Annual commitment plus a discount creates a psychological and financial commitment that monthly billing doesn't.
</KeyData>

### How to offer it

Skool supports both monthly and annual billing. The standard approach:
- Monthly: your regular price (e.g., $29/mo)
- Annual: a discount equivalent to 2-3 months free (e.g., $249/year instead of $348)

<Insight>
The discount needs to feel meaningful. 10% off isn't compelling enough to change behavior. 2-3 months free (17-25% off) is the sweet spot where the savings feel real without destroying your revenue.
</Insight>

### When to introduce annual

<WatchOut>
Don't offer annual billing from day one. Wait until you have:
- At least 3 months of consistent content and live sessions
- Enough proof that the community delivers ongoing value
- Members who've been with you for 2+ months and are clearly staying

The reason: annual billing locks members in, but it also locks you in. If you sell annual plans before you've proven you can deliver consistent value for 12 months, you're making a promise you haven't validated. And if members buy annual, hate it by month 3, and can't cancel, they'll resent you publicly instead of quietly leaving.
</WatchOut>

## What we don't know yet

<OpenQuestion>
**What's the actual engagement threshold that predicts churn on Skool?** We said 14+ days of silence is a warning sign, but is it 7 days? 21 days? Probably varies by community cadence. Co-pilots tracking the gap between "last activity" and "cancellation date" could pin this down.
</OpenQuestion>

<OpenQuestion>
**Does the monthly ROI post actually reduce churn?** It's logical that reminding members of value would help retention, but we don't have before/after data on this tactic. If co-pilots introduce ROI posts and track churn before and after, we'd have real evidence.
</OpenQuestion>

<OpenQuestion>
**What's the optimal outreach timing for quiet members?** We said reach out at 14+ days of silence. But maybe day 7 is better (before the habit of not-visiting solidifies). Co-pilots experimenting with different timing can compare recovery rates.
</OpenQuestion>

<OpenQuestion>
**Does annual billing work for communities under $20/mo?** The commitment logic applies at any price, but the savings amount matters psychologically. Is saving $38/year (on a $19/mo plan with 2 months free) compelling enough to change behavior? Unclear.
</OpenQuestion>
